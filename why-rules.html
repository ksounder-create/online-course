Why Rules

Science progresses only when phenomena are governed by rules.

What Rules Do

Rules define relationships. They explain why events occur, not merely that they occur.

In every mature science, rules perform three functions:

  • They make observations intelligible
  • They eliminate contradictions
  • They allow prediction and reasoning

Without rules, data remains descriptive. With rules, data becomes knowledge.

Why Biology Developed Without Rules

Biology evolved differently from physics or chemistry. Its objects of study were complex, variable, and difficult to isolate.

As a result, biology developed as a descriptive science:

  • Observations were cataloged
  • Correlations were reported
  • Mechanisms were inferred
  • Exceptions were tolerated

Over time, this approach became institutionalized. Description replaced explanation.

Why Data Alone Could Not Produce Understanding

Biology assumed that increasing data would eventually reveal governing principles.

Instead, the opposite occurred:

  • More data produced more models
  • More models produced more contradictions
  • More contradictions required more exceptions

This is not a failure of data. It is the inevitable result of working without rules.

What Rules Change

The introduction of rules changes biology fundamentally.

When rules are known:

  • Data becomes interpretable
  • Contradictions resolve themselves
  • Mechanisms become predictable
  • Exceptions disappear

Rules do not add complexity. They remove it.

The Critical Realization

Rules are not created. They are discovered.

Once discovered, they redefine the field. They determine what is possible, what is impossible, and what must be true.

This is the point at which biology becomes a rational science.
